The 2030 DRAM Squeeze: 157.5 Exabytes of Demand Meets Only 128.8 Exabytes of Supply

Global DRAM demand is projected to reach 157.5 exabytes by 2030, while base-case supply tops out at just 128.8 EB. The resulting 28.7 EB shortfall lands entirely in general DRAM — HBM supply and demand match exactly (37.5 EB vs 37.5 EB) — with agentic-AI CPUs' massive memory appetite driving the crisis.

🧩 Supply Analysis: Where the Gap Really Is

▾

Projected 2030 supply of 128.8 EB splits into two categories: General DRAM (DDR4, DDR5, LPDDR) at 91.3 EB, and HBM production at 37.5 EB.

The Real Source of the Crisis: General DRAM demand sits at 120.0 EB while producible supply reaches only 91.3 EB — the entire 28.7 EB shortfall lives here. HBM tells the opposite story: 37.5 EB of demand is matched one-to-one by 37.5 EB of supply. The 2030 memory crisis isn't an HBM problem — it's the result of standard DRAM being "cannibalized" by HBM production.

🗓️ Timeline: Short, Medium, and Long Term

▾
Short-Term (2026-2027)
Memory supply stays tight. SK Group Chairman Chey Tae-won: "until 2030 there's still some shortage." Micron CEO Sanjay Mehrotra: meaningful large-scale new capacity is unlikely before 2028; current capacity meets only 50-66% of core customer demand.
Medium-Term (2028-2029)
Gradual relief arrives as new fabs come online: the first Yongin fab reaches full capacity by H1 2030, Micron's Idaho and Singapore facilities start contributing, and Samsung's accelerated construction adds supply — a more comfortable environment for DDR5, LPDDR server memory, and HBM.
Long-Term (2030 and Beyond)
If agentic AI adoption accelerates faster than capacity expansion, the shortage could deepen; large fab projects and supplier expansions only become meaningful from 2029-2030 onward. The structural nature of the shortage suggests it won't fully resolve before the end of the decade.

🎯 Strategic Implications

▾
For Memory Manufacturers
  • Keep shifting wafer starts toward HBM and advanced DDR5 products
  • Accelerate investment in next-generation memory technologies
  • Diversify manufacturing geographically to mitigate geopolitical risk
  • Build long-term supply partnerships with key AI customers
For AI Companies
  • Secure supply to guarantee access to critical components
  • Design system architectures that optimize memory usage
  • Explore alternative memory technologies and suppliers to reduce risk
  • Account for continued memory price premiums in infrastructure budgeting
For End Users
  • Expect continued cost increases through 2030
  • Plan for longer lead times on memory-intensive systems
  • Evaluate alternative architectures less exposed to supply constraints
  • Consider strategic inventory management for critical applications
