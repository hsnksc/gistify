🌏 Global Supply Chain & Competition

Hardware control of physical AI has become a new struggle for technological sovereignty among world powers. China is the outright leader in manufacturing scale and supply-chain density; per late-2025 data, ~74–80% of global humanoid shipments came from China-based makers.

The integrated ecosystem in the Yangtze River Delta clusters motor, precision-gear, sensor and actuator makers within a two-hour logistics radius. A prototype cycle that takes 12 weeks in the West drops to 10–14 days in Shenzhen and Shanghai, giving Chinese makers record iteration speed.

⚠️ ISO 10218:2025 & the Safety Shift

Deploying humanoids into industrial settings is fundamentally reshaping insurance policies, product liability and regulation. The ISO 10218-1/2 standards updated at end-2025 are the product of an eight-year global effort, shifting the certification focus from hardware alone to the "entire collaborative application."

The floor the robot stands on, blind spots, cybersecurity vulnerabilities and the geometry of the carried load are now all part of the risk analysis. The human-robot biomechanical force limits from ISO/TS 15066 are also integrated into this standard.

🚶 Dynamic Balance & the Residual-Risk Gap

Traditional standards were built for static or wheeled (statically balanced) systems. Bipedal "dynamically balanced" humanoids constantly shift their center of gravity, performing a "controlled fall."

Fall hazard: During power loss or emergency stops, tipping over poses a risk to people and surroundings. The ISO 25785-1 standard to cover this is still at working-draft stage. The EU's Machinery Regulation (EU 2023/1230), effective 2027, places all product liability for autonomous self-learning systems directly on the manufacturer.
