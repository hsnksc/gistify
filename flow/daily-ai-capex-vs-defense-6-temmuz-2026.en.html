2.2 Real Growth and GDP Deflator: Mechanism

Nominal GDP is obtained by multiplying real GDP by the price leandl. BEA'nın kullandığı Fisher ideal indeks formülü ile hesaplanan GDP deflatör, ekonomideki tüm içsel üretimin fiyat değişimini ölçer; tüketici sepeti (CPI) andya ithalatı kapsamaz [^bea1].

Nominal GDP = Real GDP × (GDP Deflator / 100)

or approximately for growth rates:

Nominal Growth Rate ≈ Real Growth Rate + Deflator (Inflation)

In this analysis, AI CapEx and defense expenditures are expressed in nominal dollars, and the denominator is nominal GDP. Therefore, the deflator's effect is bidirectional:

  • High deflatör (yüksek enflasyon) → Nominal GSYİH daha hızlı büyür → Aynı nominal harcama GSYİH yüzdesi olarak daha küçük görünür.
  • Low deflatör (düşük enflasyon) → Nominal GSYİH daha yavaş büyür → Aynı nominal harcama GSYİH yüzdesi olarak daha büyük görünür.

5. Line Item Analysis: Where Is This Money Going?

5.1 Nuclear Triad Modernization — $946B (2025–2034)

The CBO estimates the operating and modernization cost of nuclear forces for 2025–2034 at $946B (annual average ~$95B). This is 25% higher than the 2023 estimate [^cbo_nuclear]. Columbia-class SSBN ($16.2B/yr), B-21 Raider ($6.1B), LGM-35 Sentinel ($4.6B) and LRSO ($1.5B) programs are at the center of this budget. The Sentinel ICBM program experienced an additional $24B increase due to a Nunn-McCurdy breach (cost overrun) [^cbo_nuclear]. According to CRS, the DOD FY2026 budget request sought "~$60B" for the nuclear enterprise [^crs_nuclear]. The difference between the annual $56B (DOD statement) and $95B (CBO total nuclear cost) stems from the DOE/NNSA share and operational costs.

5.2 Space Force — 80% Growth ($71B FY2027)

Space Force, FY2027 ichin $71B talep ediyor — bu, FY2026 $40B bütçesine göre %80'lik bir artış [^spaceforce1]. RDT&E $40.6B (previous $29B), procurement $19B (previous $3.6B — 5x increase). Golden Dome ($17.5B total) is financed from the Space Force portfolio. However, in the OMB's 5-year plan (FYDP), the Space Force budget falls to $69B in 2028 and $65B in 2030 — showing the reconciliation increase is not permanent [^spaceforce1].

5.3 Navy 355/390-Gemilik Filo — $40B/Year İnşa

CBO, Navy'nin 2025 gemi inşa planının 30 yılda annual average $40B (2024 sabit dolar) maliyetli olacağını tahmin ediyor — bu mevcut 5 annual averagenın %46 üzeri [^cbo_navy]. 390 savaş gemisi hedefi ichin Columbia SSBN and Virginia SSN aynı tersanelerde (HII, GD Electric Boat) üretiliyor — darboğaz riski yüksek. 18 combat ships + 16 auxiliary ships are requested for FY2027 ($65.8B procurement) [^insidedef].

5.4 Drone / Autonomous Systems — $63B (6x Increase)

The FY2027 defense budget allocates $63B to drone and autonomous technology — a 6x increase over the FY2026 base of $10.5B. The Drone Dominance Program targets 200,000+ autonomous systems [^drone1]. According to DefenseScoop, the Pentagon is requesting a total of $70B+ for autonomous systems ($53.6B) and counter-drone ($21B). DAWG (Defense Autonomous Warfare Group) budget soars from $226M to $54.6B (24,000% increase) [^defscoop1].

$The $63B drone request represents a shift in US defense doctrine toward "attritable mass over exquisite survivability" — mass producibility over expensive survivability. Ukrayna and Mediumdoğu deneyimleri, drone savaşının maliyet-etkinliğini kanıtladı. However, the 200,000-unit target requires 10–15x capacity increase from current US industry — meaning 2–4 years of procurement delay [^drone1].

5.5 Personnel Costs — $600B Structural Pressure

The CBO notes that in the President's 2025 budget request, total military compensation (including veterans) is $600B — a 162% real increase since 1980 [^cbo_personnel]. Cost per active duty member ~$140,000. Askeri emeklilik and sağlık harcamaları (TRICARE) bütçeyi zorluyor. One-third of the DOD budget is personnel [^cbo_personnel].

CSBA analisti Todd Harrison, personel maliyetlerini "the 600-pound gorilla" olarak nitelendiriyor — if current trends continue, personnel costs will consume the entire defense budget by 2039 [^goandxec1]. Today's soldier count is 30% lower than 1980, but costs are much higher. This limits resources available for modernization (ships, aircraft, drones).

10.1 Global Power Demand: A Tripling Hunger

Data center electricity consumption has grown four times faster than global total consumption since 2017, recording an average annual increase of 12% [^iea1]. In 2023, global data center demand was approximately 415 TWh, while in 2025 it reached 485 TWh according to the IEA and 447 TWh according to Gartner [^gart1][^iea1]. This increase is directly related to the exponentially growing demand for AI-specific servers.

Forecasts for the next five years reveal the sector's unpredictable scale. According to the IEA April 2026 report, global data center electricity consumption will reach 950 TWh by 2030, approximately double the 2025 level, accounting for 3% of global total demand [^iea1]. Gartner paints a more aggressive scenario: 565 TWh in 2026, and over 1,200 TWh in 2030 — equivalent to Japan's entire annual electricity consumption [^gart1]. On the capacity side, Goldman Sachs targets 122 GW of new capacity for 2030, while McKinsey offers 219 GW and Deloitte expects 176 GW by 2035 [^gs1][^mck1][^del1]. JPMorgan has calculated that 122 GW of new capacity needs to be commissioned between 2026-2030, requiring over $5 trillion in investment over 5 years, with a $1.4 trillion financing gap [^jpm1].

To see that this increase is not just a forecast but a physical reality straining infrastructure, one only needs to look at US data centers. In 2025, approximately half of the total US demand growth came from data centers [^fort1]. According to the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory December 2024 report, US data center consumption reached 176 TWh in 2023; by 2028 it is expected to expand to a band of 325-580 TWh [^doe1]. In other words, demand will double or triple within five years in the US.

Grid constraints in regions where data center demand is concentrated reached "critical" and "binding" levels as of 2025. The geographic distribution of these constraints is as follows:

PJM (Mid-Atlantic / Northern Virginia "Data Center Alley")

PJM, the largest US electricity market, has entered a capacity crisis due to the direct impact of data center demand. According to the Luminix April 2026 report, data centers alone account for 97% of PJM's 5,250 MW load increase forecast [^lum1]. Capacity prices have risen 833% to $270/MW-day, then hit the FERC cap of $333/MW-day; despite this, the auction still remains 6,517 MW short [^lum1]. PJM has proposed a 14.9 GW emergency supply process, with a FERC decision expected in June 2026 [^lum1].

In the Dominion Energy region alone, 30+ GW of data center demand is queued by 2030; Dominion is preparing for 70,000 MW (70 GW) of new demand [^enk1]. The interconnection queue has accumulated 60+ GW, with an average wait time of 4 years and peaks reaching 7 years [^int1].

ERCOT (Texas)

Texas has broken the US record in terms of absolute queue size: as of April 2026, 410 GW of large load requests are in queue, with 87% of this from data centers [^erc1]. In the Oncor grid alone, 650 requests point to a total 273 GW load accumulation [^erc1]. The queue doubled from 169,615 MW to 410,618 MW in 10 months [^erc1]. However, only 1.8% of this queue is actually energized [^lum1]. Texas Senate Bill SB6 (2025) introduced financial commitment requirements for loads of 75+ MW and cleared approximately 40% of the queue of speculative projects by 2026 [^erc1].

10.4 Cooling: Liquid Cooling Requirement and Water Scarcity

The power density of AI data centers has brought cooling technology to a mandatory inflection point. While traditional racks consume 10-15 kW, AI training racks reached 40-70 kW as of 2024, and systems like NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 reach 100-132 kW, climbing to 240 kW/rack within a year [^del1][^liq1]. GPU TDP (Thermal Design Power) per unit climbs from 700W to 1,000W+ (GB200/GB300) and 2,300W (Rubin) [^tone1].

This density makes air cooling physically impossible. Traditional air cooling operates at PUE ~1.5 but is inadequate above 20 kW/rack. Direct-to-chip liquid cooling delivers PUE <=1.15, 1,600W+ capacity per component, and when operated in a closed-loop, water loss is minimal [^liq1][^tone1]. The NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 is 25 times more energy efficient and 300 times more water efficient than traditional air cooling [^nv1].

The liquid cooling market will grow from $2.8 billion in 2025 to over $21 billion in 2032 (30%+ CAGR) [^liq1]. AWS has committed to using recycled wastewater in 120 US data centers by 2030 [^enk3]. Microsoft has adopted liquid cooling as the standard architecture from 2025 onward [^dq1].

Water scarcity is the number one source of data center opposition: in more than 40% of controversial projects, water usage is the main opposition argument [^dcw1]. In Arizona, Tract's $14 billion project was withdrawn in May 2024 due to water and resource concerns [^dcw1]. Loudoun County, VA (Data Center Alley) is experiencing a massive construction boom in a region with periodic drought risk.

10.5 Construction and Equipment: Fatal Mismatch

The mismatch between data center construction time and power infrastructure development time is the sector's most structural constraint. While a data center can be built in 12-24 months, grid connection takes 3-7 years, and in some regions 7-10 years [^avis1][^arch1]. The Archdesk April 2026 report summarizes: "this mismatch is not marginal; the grid queue time is three to four times the construction schedule. In this gap, announced projects die and construction contracts are practically suspended pending completion" [^arch1].

The equipment supply chain crisis deepens this mismatch. Large power transformer delivery times have risen from 18-24 months in 2024 to 36-48 months (128 weeks average) in 2026. Generator step-up transformers are at 144 weeks, high-voltage cables at 18-24 months, switchgear at 12-18 months [^int3][^arch1]. Approximately half a dozen US data center constructions have been completed but cannot be activated because transformers cannot be found [^int3].

The cost of this timeline mismatch is substantial. In a 60 MW facility, a one-month delay means $14.2 million in lost revenue (~$3.55 million per week) [^arch1]. Integrated System Testing (IST) takes 4-6 weeks for a standard air-cooled hall, and 10-14 weeks for a liquid-cooled AI hall [^arch1].

PPA market: In the Dominion region, solar PPAs are priced at a 17.5% premium to the PJM composite value; this gap has risen from 11.6% the previous year [^pex1]. Renewable contract values occasionally exceed the $100/MWh threshold [^pex1]. The PPA platform market will grow from $2.4 billion in 2025 to $9.5 billion in 2035 (14.6% CAGR) [^gmi1].

10.6 Carbon and Jevons Paradox: Efficiency, But More Consumption

In February 2026, Microsoft announced that it has procured a total of 40 GW of renewable energy across 26 countries with 400+ contracts and 95+ companies, with 19 GW online, declaring it has reached its "100% renewable electricity" target [^ms2]. However, this target masks the difference between market-based accounting (Renewable Energy Certificate purchases) and location-based accounting (actual physical consumption).

According to TheEnergy.co May 2026 analysis, the location-based emissions of Microsoft, Meta, and Alphabet have increased by 72% or more between 2021-2024 — despite their 100% renewable claims [^tec1]. Companies buy RECs from solar farms hundreds of kilometers away while physically drawing from coal-heavy grids at midnight. Location-based emissions can be 4,000 times higher than market-based emissions [^tec1].

The global data center fuel mix (physical, not contractual) is: coal ~30%, renewable ~27%, natural gas ~26%, nuclear ~15%. Coal and natural gas together will cover more than 40% of additional data center demand by 2030 [^iea2]. Absolute coal consumption for data centers will only begin to decline after 2035 once SMRs scale up [^iea2].

PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) Plateau: In modern air-cooled data centers, PUE is in the 1.3-1.5 band; in liquid-cooled hyperscale facilities, it is in the 1.1-1.15 range. However, hyperscale PUE has plateaued at 1.1; there is very little room for further overhead energy reduction [^arxiv1]. PUE also only measures facility efficiency, not IT equipment efficiency. A facility with PUE 1.05 running 100 MW of AI GPUs still has massive absolute energy consumption.

Jevons Paradox — Critical Takeaway: Technological efficiency improvements reduce the unit cost of a resource, thereby increasing total consumption. Google's Gemini optimization has reduced service cost per unit by 78% [^usr1]. But this means more users, more queries, larger models, and more training. Efficiency gains are reinvested into growth.

According to MIT thesis and arXiv 2024-2025 studies, the rebound effect in the ICT sector is estimated at 115-161%; consumption increases have outpaced efficiency gains [^mit1][^arxiv1]. Between 2010-2018, data center workloads grew 550% while electricity consumption only rose 6%; this was a period when demand was limited by human attention and market constraints. But since 2022, AI models have exhibited "near-infinite demand": there is no natural saturation point as long as compute yields better performance [^arxiv1]. Microsoft's own data confirms this: total electricity consumption, which was 10.8 million MWh in FY20, rose to 29.8 million MWh in FY24 (176% increase); in North America, a 136% increase was recorded [^ms3].

Verdict: Efficiency gains will be reinvested into larger models, more users, more inference, and more agents. As unit efficiency increases, total energy demand will also increase. The Jevons Paradox is a structural constraint showing that AI alone cannot solve the energy problem.

Microsoft's 2030 carbon negative target is at risk: TheEnergy.co has reported that Microsoft is looking to abandon its "100% time-matched renewable" plan, citing AI's exponential growth as the reason [^tec1].

11.8 National Security Impacts: What Does AI CapEx > Defense Mean?

Dual-Use Nature

AI data center infrastructure can also be used directly for military purposes. The Pentagon's Joint Warfighting Cloud Capability and JADC2 networks share the same physical infrastructure with commercial AI data centers [^38]. In March 2026, Iran targeted AWS facilities in the UAE and Bahrain as military assets [^38]. This situation strengthens the argument that part of AI CapEx indirectly serves defense infrastructure. But this also means that private sector investments are directed by market dynamics, independent of military units' budget discipline.

Neoprimes: The New Actors of the Defense Industry

The Pentagon's new actors in the defense industry — Palantir, Anduril, OpenAI, SpaceX — are called "neoprimes" (new heavy industry) [^19]. These companies offer software-defined, AI-centric solutions against the traditional Lockheed Martin/Boeing duopoly. Palantir earned $1.57 billion in US government revenue in 2024 (45% increase) [^19]. Anduril has a $20 billion ceiling Army C-UAS contract with the Lattice platform [^19]. SpaceX is executing a $2.29 billion Space Force contract with Starshield [^18].

These companies are dependent not on the defense budget but on private capital markets (VC, IPO, debt); this carries the risk of military capabilities becoming sensitive to financial market cycles.

Technological Superiority = Strategic Priority

The White House's July 2025 AI Action Plan states: "It is a national security imperative for the US to achieve unquestionable and unchallengeable global technological superiority" [^37]. This means that technological superiority is defined as a separate and superior strategic priority from traditional defense spending. The industrial base strategy now encompasses not only tank and aircraft production, but also AI chip production capacity, cloud computing backbone, data center energy security, and rare earth element supply chains [^37].

250x Gap: A Conclusion Assessment

The Big Tech AI CapEx ($725B) / DoD AI budget (~$3B) = ~250x ratio concretizes the gap between the resources the US uses to develop its military capabilities and the resources it spends to achieve technological superiority. This ratio shows that defense is not outside the AI revolution, but is largely second in resource allocation at a massive scale. DAWG's $54.6 billion budget and Sentinel's $140 billion 10-year cost pale in comparison to a single hyperscaler's (e.g., Amazon's $200 billion in 2026) annual AI CapEx.

This asymmetry carries two opposite risks: (1) military AI capabilities become dependent on private sector market winds; (2) the risk of over-investment (overbuild), as JPMorgan noted, can create a financial bubble with the $40 billion annual amortization vs. $15-20 billion revenue imbalance [^38]. GPU lifespan is 3-5 years; military systems have 20-30 year life cycles, which is the opposite [^38].

Conclusion: The US has positioned technological superiority as a more strategic priority than traditional defense in 2026-2027. This choice may provide a long-term advantage in the great power competition with China; however, in the short term, it creates resource scarcity in traditional security areas such as military readiness, NATO resilience, and nuclear modernization. The 250x gap concretizes the scale of this choice numerically.

Section 16: Risks and Uncertainties

In 2026, five hyperscalers' guided AI CapEx is ~$725B while direct AI revenue is only measured at $50-60B [^si1]. In the telecom bubble, 85% overbuild and $2T in losses occurred [^si1]; a similar scenario in AI infrastructure shows that 30-50% of GPU clusters and data centers may face underutilization. The critical epistemic gap: there is no public GPU utilization data analysis — CreditSights, Moody's, JPMorgan, McKinsey, Gartner, MIT, CBRE, JLL and dozens of institutions do not contain this data [^cross11]. This silence either indicates that low utilization is shameful, or that the industry is so fragmented that not even a standard metric exists.

2. Financial Sustainability

Hyperscalers' total FCF has collapsed to ~$4B in 2026 — the lowest level since 2014, while revenue has grown 7x [^cross9]. Section 17: Conclusion and Recommendations

The central thesis of this report — that AI CapEx will exceed US defense spending in 2027 — is robustly validated across all GDP scenarios of the CBO, IMF, Fed, and OMB, below the CBO defense baseline [^insight7]. The AI %GDP ratio reaches ~3.3% in 2027 while defense declines to ~2.7%. This may be the largest strategic reallocation in the US since the Cold War. But this historic turning point is not an "inevitable growth" story; it is a "scenario-dependent gamble." Below are concrete, actionable recommendations for five different target audiences.

Diversification strategy: Cap-weight S&P 500 (SPY) is a concentration trap where 35% is formed by 10 AI-heavy stocks. Equal-weight S&P 500 (RSP) beat SPY in 2026; this is the signal that the market is silently hedging against AI concentration risk [^insight11]. Risk management: Bond spreads and hyperscaler FCFs should be monitored weekly. Amazon FCF negative, Meta $30B bond, Oracle $100B+ debt — deterioration in these metrics could be a precursor to a credit event [^cross9]. The distinction between core cloud (Amazon, Microsoft, Google) and speculative AI (CoreWeaand, neoclouds) is critical: in the speculative segment, D/E 4.5x, revenue concentration 62-67% to Microsoft [^insight4].

Core cloud vs speculative AI: A clear distinction should be made between hyperscalers with strong FCF, low debt, and competitive moat (Amazon AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud) and neoclouds dependent on NVIDIA financing, single-customer (Microsoft), and off-balance-sheet structures.

2. Recommendations for Policymakers

National AI strategy: The US does not have a "national AI strategy" — while the private sector (Big Tech) spends $725B+, DoD AI budget is $3B [^insight3]. This 200-250x gap creates a strategic vulnerability. A national AI strategy should coordinate military and commercial AI development; the JADC2 2.0 framework should integrate private sector capabilities.

Regulatory framework: EO 14365's 7-pillar framework (security, safety, privacy, justice, competition, freedom, innovation) is interpreted differently in 50 states [^phase1]. A consistent federal AI law at the federal level reduces uncertainty and encourages long-term investment. A US-EU transatlantic AI regulatory corridor compatible with the EU AI Act could shape the global standard according to US interests.

International cooperation / AI diplomacy: AI offers an opportunity for cooperation in a non-military field. The US-China AI competition could be softened through "AI diplomacy" in areas such as climate modeling, health, and agriculture. NATO standardization with allies is an area of cooperation beyond the 3.5% defense target debate [^insight12].

3. Recommendations for Companies

Efficiency-focused AI: Google's Gemini model reduced costs by 78% [^insight5]. This shows that the scale-before-efficiency logic is changing. Obsolescence risk management: The 12-18 month GPU cycle collides with 3-5 year depreciation. Companies should financially hedge this risk with accelerated depreciation policies and reservation flexibility.

Anthropic's refusal to conduct Pentagon supply chain risk assessment [^insight3] shows the fragility of this coordination. Supply chain security and industry incentives can increase private sector-public cooperation.

Drone vs nuclear balance: The $63B drone budget exceeds Germany's entire defense budget ($58B) [^phase1]. But the $946B nuclear triad is a Cold War relic. In the US-China AI race, attritable autonomous systems may be more effective than strategic weapons platforms.

5. Recommendations for Global Society

AI access: Open-source models like DeepSeek, Llama, and Mistral enable the spread of AI capabilities to developing countries. Developed countries should guide this open-source ecosystem with security and quality standards rather than restricting it.

Data privacy: AI's data needs conflict with personal privacy. The GDPR and EU AI Act are efforts to establish this balance. A global "AI data rights" framework could prevent digital colonialism.

Climate-aware computing: AI data centers are a rapidly growing source of global emissions. Instead of renewable claims, "carbon label" standards — requiring disclosure of the carbon footprint of every AI output — enable consumers to make informed choices.

Developing countries: AI infrastructure is creating a new digital divide. The World Bank and IMF could create private financing mechanisms for AI infrastructure ("AI development bonds" similar to green bonds). Otherwise, AI benefits will remain in the US, China, and Europe; the remaining 6 billion people will be excluded.

General Conclusion

The AI CapEx > defense intersection will occur in 2027 — this is mathematically robust but a scenario-dependent outcome. Under the CBO defense baseline, 2027 is certain; if Trump's $1.5T defense request passes, it could be 2028 or never occur at all [^cross1]. But the intersection itself carries a deeper meaning: the US, through its private sector, is allocating more resources to artificial intelligence infrastructure than to military security. This is a paradigm shift — but it could also be a bubble.

Most likely scenario (40-50% probability): "Bubble + paradigm" — AI CapEx peaks in 2027-2028, followed by a correction (40-44% collapse in rental prices, some neocloud bankruptcies, bond spread widening). But after the correction, surviving hyperscalers and efficiency winners (like the Gemini -78% model) build a more sustainable AI economy. This scenario parallels 2000 dot-com: NASDAQ fell 78%, but Amazon, Google, and eBay survived and grew larger.

Worse scenario (25-30%): "Solow + bubble" — the J-curve never arrives, the productivity paradox continues, $5T investment becomes "stranded asset," a credit event erupts in 2028-2029. Telecom bubble: $2T loss, 85% overbuild.

Better scenario (20-25%): "J-curve + paradigm" — AI productivity gains explode in 2028-2030, contributing +1.0 pp+ to GDP growth, $5T investment truly becomes cheap. 1995-2005 IT boom: 10-year delay, but the result was tremendous.

This report was written in the middle of the largest single industrial investment wave in history. $725B+ annual AI CapEx is 3.1 times the Apollo + Marshall + Manhattan Plan [^phase1]. But this investment is a blind bet: GPU utilization rate is unknown, the revenue-expense balance is very wide, financial sustainability is questioned, geopolitical fragility is high. The message for investors, policymakers, corporate executives, and defense planners is clear: the AI revolution is real, but the journey is more important than the destination. The risks are great, the opportunities are great. This report aims to provide a map for that journey.
