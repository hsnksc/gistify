🧭 Methodology Summary and Glossary

This report treats the question “is AI a bubble?” not as a binary yes/no question but as a layered systemic-risk question. The core premise: financial fragility and operational/systemic importance are not the same thing. A company can be both financially rock-solid and the single most critical link in the system (TSMC); or financially fragile yet largely substitutable in operational terms (CoreWeave). Melting these two dimensions into a single score is misleading.

The report therefore separates four types of risk:

1 · Financial default risk

The probability a company cannot service its debt/obligations. Metrics: leverage, interest coverage, liquidity, maturity concentration.

2 · Operational supply disruption

The probability a company stops delivering products/services. Metrics: production concentration, substitution time, geographic risk.

3 · Financial contagion risk

The degree to which a failure spreads via credit, collateral and market channels. Metrics: connectedness, collateral structure, counterparty concentration.

4 · Market / valuation risk

A decline in the share price; a type of loss independent of default. Shareholders can suffer large losses without a bankruptcy.

Measurement runs on three separate axes, and none is multiplied into a single pseudo-precise score: (A) Systemic Criticality — irreplaceability, connectedness, substitution time, economic criticality of the service, geographic concentration; (B) Failure Probability — net leverage, interest/fixed-charge coverage, liquidity, debt-maturity concentration, floating-rate exposure, operational and geopolitical risk; (C) Expected Systemic Loss — a calibrated qualitative joint assessment of the first two plus a contagion coefficient (calibrated judgment, not an arithmetic product).

Verbal probability classes are tied to a transparent numeric band (§2.6); a base and a positive scenario are added alongside the adverse ones (§8.6–8.7); a cross-scenario conditional-probability and intersection matrix is built (§8.8); the conclusion presents good-path / bad-path probabilities through a decision tree (§11.1); and §13 shows how the scenarios translate into premium-only long call and put portfolios with model examples. Sources are ranked by quality tier: official filing → rating agency → company statement → analyst estimate → media.

🎯 1. Executive Summary

The AI value chain sits at the center of the largest capital-expenditure cycle in tech history, operating on increasingly difficult macroeconomic ground. This report maps the chain's six layers — lithography (ASML), foundry (TSMC, GlobalFoundries, Intel), chip design (NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom, Qualcomm), data center/network/energy (Arista, Super Micro, Constellation), hyperscalers (Amazon, Meta, Alphabet, Microsoft, Apple) and the edge layer (neo-cloud: CoreWeave, Nebius, Lambda; LLM: OpenAI, Anthropic) — in terms of financial resilience and systemic importance.

Core judgment: “Is AI a bubble?” has no one-word answer. The chain is a stack of layers with very different risk profiles. The right question is: which link in the chain is fragile, how far does contagion spread when that link snaps, and which layer is genuinely irreplaceable?

Five key findings

1 — Systemic criticality and financial risk diverge in opposite directions. The chain's most irreplaceable links (TSMC, ASML, NVIDIA) also carry the strongest balance sheets: TSMC's interest coverage is 242.5x2, cash-to-debt 3.1712; ASML's debt-to-equity is 13%3. By contrast, the financially most fragile links (CoreWeave, Super Micro, Intel) are largely substitutable operationally. This observation rests on a clear divergence within the sample studied — not on a statistical correlation — and it shows the intuition “most indebted = most dangerous” is wrong: a debt crisis does not stop the system; a supply shock does.

2 — The most likely near-term break is financial; the most destructive long-term break is physical. The most likely first break sits in the highly leveraged, thin-margin neo-cloud layer (CoreWeave: $25.1B debt, current ratio 0.31, S&P 'B' rating4,5). But that layer's failure does not stop the system; credit funds book losses and assets migrate at a discount to the hyperscalers. The most destructive scenario is the physical loss of TSMC — low probability but irreparable. These two cannot be presented as “most dangerous” in the same sentence; one is most likely, the other most destructive — and the report keeps that distinction.

3 — “Mortgaging the future” is not one thing but three different mechanisms. Borrowing (Amazon's bond issuance reaching $72B in 20266), non-cancellable purchase commitments (Meta's $238B binding contracts, $107B in a single quarter7) and equity dilution (Alphabet's $84.75B share issuance8) are not the same economic category. Debt carries interest and rollover risk; commitments create irreversible obligations; an equity raise strengthens the balance sheet and shifts risk to equity instead of debt. The report measures these with separate metrics rather than one “mortgage index” (§7.4).

4 — LLM revenue generation does not yet cover the physical cost — but it is not the only decisive variable. ARR at the top of the system (LLMs) does not yet finance the physical capex at the bottom: OpenAI's 2026 cash burn is estimated at $27B (analyst estimate44), while Anthropic's net cash position is not public. Yet the claim that “the game's continuation depends on a single variable — LLM ROI” is over-centralization: hyperscalers have cross-subsidy capacity from advertising, e-commerce and enterprise software; GPUs are not used solely for foundation-model training; power capacity, custom ASICs and chip efficiency are independent critical variables. The right question: by what date can AI-attributable incremental cash flow cover incremental capex and fixed obligations?

5 — The chronology of breakage: financial first, physical later. In order of probability: (i) neo-cloud credit default (most likely, limited impact), (ii) hyperscaler capex-discipline crisis (medium probability, broad market impact), (iii) physical loss of TSMC (lowest probability, catastrophic impact). Investor and regulator focus should shift from profit margins to hidden leverage mechanisms — CoreWeave's DDTL covenant thresholds, GPU-collateralized loans and undisclosed LLM churn rates — flagged as unknowns in §10.

What this report does and does not do

Through scenarios and stress tests, the report answers which domino topples the others, what conditions everyone needs to stay in the game, and which companies can fail even without the whole sector deteriorating. It also: ties verbal probability classes to a transparent numeric band (§2.6), adds a base and a positive scenario alongside the adverse ones (§8.6–8.7), builds the cross-scenario conditional-probability and intersection matrix (§8.8), delivers good-path vs. bad-path probabilities via a decision tree (§11) and converts the scenarios into premium-only long call/put portfolios (§13). What the report does not do: present unverifiable data as certain, dress numeric bands up as an empirically calibrated model, or count planned financing as cash on hand.

2.2 Four risk types kept separate

These four risk types are routinely conflated in analysis; this report ties every claim to the risk type it belongs to (see the methodology cards above). The practical consequence of the distinction: Arista's zero debt makes it low-risk in terms of financial default, but not “risk-free” — customer concentration, revenue decline and valuation (market) risk remain. The report avoids absolute statements (“risk-free”, “cannot fail”) and instead names the risk type and the metric.

2.3 Three measurement axes (no multiplication)

A common approach reduces systemic risk to a single multiplicative formula such as “irreplaceability × connectedness × leverage”. That formula contradicts its own conclusion: since TSMC's leverage is near zero, a true product would push its systemic score toward zero — yet TSMC is considered the most critical. The fix is to separate the concepts: Axis A (what does the system lose if the company stops?), Axis B (how likely is the company to stop?) and Axis C (expected systemic loss — expressed through the two-axis matrix in §4 and the qualitative priority ranking in §5, not an arithmetic product; no numeric multiplication without defined scale, normalization and weights).

2.4 Shock direction: upstream / downstream

Calling the LLM layer the “top” and the production layer the “bottom”, then saying “demand shocks strike from the bottom up”, is self-contradictory. This report defines three flows with separate arrows, in neutral upstream/downstream terminology:

  • Physical product and capacity: ASML → TSMC → NVIDIA → data center → LLM (flows downstream).
  • Cash: customer/LLM → cloud → hardware → foundry → equipment (flows upstream).
  • Demand shock: end demand → suppliers (propagates upstream; if LLM revenue falls, the shock travels toward ASML).

2.5 Confidence tiers and labeling rules

  • High confidence (fact) official financials, SEC filings, rating agencies, company statements → presented unlabeled, as fact.
  • Medium confidence (estimate) analyst projections, private-market reports → labeled “analyst estimate / projection”.
  • Conflict / uncertainty data that conflicts across sources → presented in balance, no single value imposed.
  • Deliberate gap (blind spot) non-public data → left as “unknown / not computable”; no figures invented.

Market caps and share prices are presented as ranges that fluctuated through July 2026; they should not be read as any single day's close.

🔗 3. Value Chain and Dependency Network

3.1 Three flows

The AI value chain is not one chain but a network of three overlapping flows. Conflating them yields the wrong answer to “which way does the shock travel?”

Flow 1 — Physical product (downstream): ASML's EUV lithography machines go to TSMC's fabs; TSMC's leading-edge wafers go into NVIDIA, AMD and Broadcom designs; those chips combine with Arista's networking gear and Super Micro's servers into data centers; from there into hyperscaler and neo-cloud capacity; finally reaching LLM training and inference workloads.

Flow 2 — Cash (upstream): LLM developers (OpenAI, Anthropic) and enterprise customers pay rental/usage fees to cloud and neo-cloud providers; those providers pay NVIDIA/Arista/Super Micro for hardware; NVIDIA pays TSMC for production; TSMC pays ASML for machines. Cash flows in the reverse direction of the physical product.

Flow 3 — Demand shock (upstream propagation): A drop in end demand (LLM revenue, enterprise AI budgets) hits the tenant first (neo-cloud/hyperscaler), then the hardware makers (NVIDIA, Arista, SMCI), then the foundry (TSMC), and last the equipment maker (ASML).

3.2 Connectedness map

  • Highest TSMC (feeds all chip designers and, indirectly, the whole chain), NVIDIA (de-facto standard for training + inference hardware), ASML (machine source for all leading-edge production).
  • High Hyperscalers (sell capacity to LLMs and consume it for their own products), Broadcom (semiconductors + infrastructure software).
  • Medium Arista (data-center networking), Constellation (energy capacity), AMD.
  • Low / substitutable Super Micro (server assembly; Dell, HPE etc. can substitute), GlobalFoundries (legacy nodes), neo-clouds (capacity providers; hyperscalers and other neo-clouds can substitute).

3.3 Circular financing risk

Circularity: NVIDIA sells chips; neo-clouds borrow against those chips as collateral to buy more chips; LLMs rent that capacity but do not yet generate the revenue to cover its cost.10 The system is built on today's debt financing of the future revenue expected at the top of the chain (LLMs). This is classic rollover fragility: self-reinforcing while things go well, reversing when demand or credit conditions break in a single link.

Important note: This circularity does not mean the whole chain drowns in debt. The foundational layers (ASML, TSMC, NVIDIA, Arista) fund themselves with strong operating cash flow; the debt spiral is concentrated in hyperscaler capex, neo-cloud loans and LLM funding rounds.

3.4 Energy: the chain's new physical bottleneck

A seventh, increasingly critical dimension has joined the value chain: electric capacity. The physical wall in front of data-center construction is increasingly grid interconnection and power supply rather than chip supply. Nuclear/clean-energy providers like Constellation have therefore become integral to the chain (2026 revenue target of $36.5B after the Calpine acquisition11). Even with strong demand, electricity and grid capacity can cap growth — treated as a separate scenario in §8.

🗺️ 4. Systemic Criticality Matrix

4.1 Two-axis matrix: probability ≠ impact

A single-score “systemic risk” ranking melts two different concepts — a company's probability of failing and the damage its failure inflicts on the system — into one axis, putting a near-zero-debt company like TSMC on the same list as highly leveraged ones. Here the two are separated: the horizontal axis is failure probability (financial + operational + geopolitical stress, Axis B); the vertical axis is systemic impact of failure (irreplaceability, connectedness, substitution time, service criticality, geographic concentration, Axis A).

The matrix's core lesson: the most dangerous point of the system is not the biggest or most profitable company. The company that is “unlikely to fail but irreplaceable if it does” and the company that is “likely to fail but whose failure does not stop the system” must be managed separately.

Two corner cases: TSMC and CoreWeave

  • TSMC (top-left, high impact / low probability). With 242.5x interest coverage and 3.17 cash-to-debt2,12, its probability of financial failure is very low on the debt-service metrics examined. But its position in leading-edge (7nm and below) production makes it extremely hard to replace; it sits at the very top of the systemic-impact axis. The main driver of its failure probability is not financial but geopolitical/physical (§8.3).
  • CoreWeave (bottom-right, high probability / low-to-mid impact). Current ratio 0.31, net loss margin 36%, GPU-collateralized debt structure4,5 — relatively high probability of financial breakage. As a capacity provider it is partly substitutable (hyperscalers and other neo-clouds can absorb the capacity); on the systemic-impact axis it sits far below TSMC. Its collapse would question NVIDIA's pricing power via the collateral-value spiral and spread to credit funds (§8.1) — not zero impact — but it would not stop physical production.

This divergence is the report's central finding: financial fragility and operational/systemic importance are not the same thing and must not underpin the same investment/risk decision.

💣 5. Financial Fragility Matrix

Independent of the systemic-impact analysis in §4, this section measures only financial resilience: interest coverage, liquidity, leverage, debt maturity and floating-rate exposure. The question: “which company cannot roll its debt load?” A financially fragile company does not stop the system (§4) — but it spreads losses to its creditors, suppliers and the financial market.

5.1 Interest coverage: sustainability of debt service

Because capex/FCF produces economically unstable results as FCF approaches zero (§7.2), this section uses the interest coverage ratio (EBIT / interest expense) as the primary metric. Below 1.0x means operating profit does not even cover interest.

5.3 Leverage and maturity concentration

  • Floating-rate risk (high): CoreWeave's $8.5B DDTL 4.0 facility is priced between SOFR + 2.25% and a fixed 5.9%.4 In a higher-for-longer environment (§8.4) interest expense can inflate with market rates — and since margin-call thresholds are not public, the full risk is not computable (§10).
  • Maturity / refinancing risk (high): CoreWeave carries $25.1B of total debt against ~$12.6B of annual revenue and a 36% net loss margin.4 Its ability to roll debt depends on the second-hand price of the pledged GPUs and credit funds' risk appetite.
  • Structural leverage (medium): Meta has reported $238B in binding contracts ($107B of “non-cancellable” commitments in a single quarter).19 Not debt, but committed future cash outflow (§7.4); combined with a −$24B FCF projection for 2027 (analyst estimate20) it creates payment-schedule pressure.
  • Large but manageable: Amazon borrowed $72B in 20266; Fitch rates it AA-, investment grade21, so refinancing risk is low.

5.4 Financial fragility ranking

By financial fragility (independent of §4 systemic impact) — from most fragile to most solid:

  2. Super Micro — D/E 115%, net margin 3.70%; the leverage + thin-margin combination.
  3. Intel — interest coverage 0.63x; structural profitability problem (P/E −175.3x14).
  4. Meta — not debt, but a massive cash commitment + negative FCF projection.
  5. Amazon — heavy borrowing but investment-grade rating and strong operating cash.

Key distinction: This ranking answers “which company is more financially fragile?” — not “whose failure collapses the system?” The answer to the second question is in §4, and the two lists are deliberately different.

🧮 7. Corrected Financial Metrics

This section corrects ratios that can be used misleadingly and replaces each with its methodologically sound counterpart. The goal is to avoid comparing companies in the wrong units.

7.1 Why ARR ≠ cash burn

ARR is the annualized revenue run-rate at the end of a period; cash burn is net cash outflow during a period. They are different economic concepts and cannot be compared directly. “ARR growth must outpace burn” is dimensionally wrong (one is a revenue level/rate, the other a cash deficit).

OpenAI example: ARR of $25B against an estimated 2026 cash burn of ~$27B (analyst estimate44). This does not mean “ARR < burn, they'll fail” — revenue not included in ARR, one-off capex inflating burn, and funding rounds change the picture. The right question: is gross contribution margin positive and is there a path to operating cash flow? Since that data is not public, the verdict for OpenAI is “not computable” (§7.5). Metrics to use instead: gross contribution margin, OCF, FCF, monthly net cash burn, burn multiple, net revenue retention, churn, unit economics per GPU-hour.

7.2 Why capex/FCF is not a core metric

FCF is what remains of operating cash flow (OCF) after capex. Dividing capex by FCF therefore produces a ratio that mathematically explodes as FCF approaches zero — economically unstable.

Amazon example: A forward 2026 capex estimate (~$200B) cannot be mixed with trailing-12-month FCF ($1.2B) — dividing different periods misleads. In the official Q1 data, trailing-12-month OCF is $148.5B and FCF $1.2B;34 hence same-period capex ≈ OCF − FCF = $147.3B. A ratio like “200 / 1.2 = 167x” explodes on period mismatch and says nothing about Amazon's real funding gap — Amazon covers $72B of borrowing with an investment-grade AA- rating21 and strong OCF; FCF nearing zero is a deliberate growth-capex choice.

The sounder alternative — capex/OCF: For Amazon, same-period OCF $148.5B, capex ≈ $147.3B → capex/OCF ≈ 99%.34 This shows “capex is consuming nearly all operating cash; the funding gap is closed with debt” without the ratio blowing up.

7.3 Net debt / EBITDA and fixed-charge coverage

  • Net debt / EBITDA: (total debt − cash) / EBITDA. High positive values signal debt-rollover pressure. CoreWeave (debt $25.1B, loss-making) is worst on this metric; TSMC/Arista/Alphabet are negative (net cash).
  • Fixed-charge coverage: (EBIT + fixed charges) / (interest + fixed charges). Includes non-cancellable commitments as “fixed charges”; Meta's $238B commitment19 becomes more visible here than in a debt ratio.
  • Liquidity bridge: (cash + undrawn committed credit) / next 12–24 months of cash needs. With a 0.31 current ratio4, CoreWeave's bridge is weak.

7.5 The correct runway formula

A crude “funding round / annual loss” both counts planned financing as cash and confuses net loss with cash burn. The correct formula:

Runway (months) = ( Current unrestricted cash + committed and available credit − minimum operating liquidity − debt/principal coming due − mandatory capex ) / monthly scenario-adjusted net cash burn

Rules: (1) net loss ≠ cash burn; (2) a funding round is not automatically all cash (primary/secondary, tranches, conditions must be separated); (3) planned IPO or credit financing is not added to liquidity until final; (4) if data is insufficient, the result is “not computable” — no ranking.

  • OpenAI: a $122B round was reported9; but the primary/secondary split, tranches and conditions are unknown; no capex/operating split of the burn. Result: not computable (interpretable only as a “very optimistic upper bound”).
  • Anthropic: net cash + burn not public. Result: not computable / insufficient data. A non-computable value cannot enter a runway ranking.
  • Lambda: $1B syndication + $350M pre-IPO financing is planned, not final;42 the ~$175M net loss is not burn.41 Result: not computable.
  • CoreWeave: the liquidity bridge must be built; with a 0.31 current ratio the short-term bridge is weak, but covenant thresholds are needed for a definitive runway (§10).

11.2 For investors

  1. Leverage alone is not a buy/sell signal. Even the system's least indebted company (Arista) is not “risk-free” — it carries customer-concentration and valuation risk. The most indebted (CoreWeave) does not stop the system but spreads losses to its creditors. Price the two different risks (financial fragility §5 / systemic impact §4) separately.
  2. “Is AI a bubble?” is the wrong question. The right one: “by what date can AI-attributable incremental cash flow cover incremental capex and fixed obligations?” — a question that does not make LLM ROI the single variable and accounts for hyperscalers' cross-subsidy capacity.
  3. Price irreplaceability, not size. TSMC's and ASML's value comes less from profits than from having no substitute. Their risk is not debt but geographic concentration and geopolitics — a different risk category in a portfolio (§2.7).
  4. Distinguish cross-subsidy capacity. Alphabet financing via equity (taking no interest-rate risk), Amazon financing with debt, and CoreWeave financing with GPU-collateralized debt are not the same “debt” category. Financing structure (§7.4) is more informative than financing size.
  5. Read probabilities as ranges, not points. Center values like 47%/53% are transparent assumptions with wide sensitivity ranges. Decisions should hinge on the early-warning indicators (§9) that reveal which end of the range is materializing — not on a single central number.

11.3 For regulators / policymakers

  1. Hidden-covenant transparency. Neo-cloud GPU-collateralized loans (CoreWeave DDTL type) are accumulating on bank balance sheets and in credit funds; covenant thresholds are not public. Total exposure to such asset-backed, floating-rate loans should be monitored — without that size, no “financial systemic risk” verdict (§2.7) can be issued.
  2. Circular-financing visibility. In the NVIDIA → neo-cloud → LLM → cloud loop, the same economic risk counted on multiple balance sheets (§3.3) can understate systemic risk. Cross-ownership and counterparty concentration should be mapped.
  3. Geographic single point of failure. The concentration of leading-edge production in a narrow geography (TSMC) is a matter of critical-infrastructure resilience, not market power. Non-Taiwan capacity diversification (US/Japan) is a strategic priority.
  4. Power-grid planning. The physical wall in front of data-center growth is increasingly electricity, not chips; grid investment and interconnection-queue management belong in the same policy frame as AI.

13.1 Application rules and maximum risk

For long calls and long puts, the maximum loss is limited to the premium paid (excluding commissions); if the option expires worthless, 100% of the premium can be lost. A long call's theoretical profit is unlimited; a long put's profit is capped as the underlying approaches zero.54,55,61 A standard US equity option typically represents 100 shares; the amounts in the tables are maximum premium budgets, not exact contract counts.55

Contracts = floor [ maximum premium budget for the position / (option premium × 100) ]

13.4 Shared position management and exit rules

  • Staggered entry: not all $10,000 on the same day; two or three tranches on days with good liquidity and tight bid-ask spreads.
  • Limit orders: near-mid limit orders instead of market orders; skip wide-spread contracts.
  • Profit taking: when a premium is up 75–100%, realize at least half; after a news-driven volatility spike, there is no obligation to carry the whole gain to expiry.
  • Thesis break: close calls if hyperscaler capex guidance and AI revenue quality clearly deteriorate; close puts on successful refinancing, strengthening liquidity or capex resilience.
  • Time management: if the thesis has not played out, do not let calls run into the final 120–150 days or puts into the final 90 days; close or roll before time decay accelerates.59
  • Expiry: ITM options can be auto-exercised; close before expiry if a 100-share obligation is unwanted.60
  • 0DTE and weeklies: unsuited to this report's 12–36-month core scenarios; they require getting the day and hour right, not just the direction.
  • Risk cap: no option selling, no margin debt. Maximum single-name premium share is 30% in both portfolios; no automatic averaging down into losing long options.
