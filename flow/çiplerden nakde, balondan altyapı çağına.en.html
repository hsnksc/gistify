🔗 01 · Unified Framework — Two Topics, One Economic Chain

▾

There is a direct link between an agent writing an email and a nuclear power deal. As an agent executes more tasks, token demand, inference load, GPU-hours, electricity consumption, and capital needs all grow together.

01 · Agent and app
The user stops chatting and starts buying finished work.
02 · Token demand
Planning, tool use, and error-retry loops raise per-task consumption.
03 · Inference capacity
Without model routing and caching, cost compounds quickly.
04 · GPU + HBM + network
The chip alone isn't enough; memory, optical networking, and power electronics are required.
05 · MW + cooling
Electricity, grid connection, water, and liquid cooling set the physical limit.
06 · Balance sheet and valuation
CapEx, debt, depreciation, and cash flow set the market price.
The real bottleneck isn't just the GPU. Even if GPUs are available, capacity can't generate revenue unless the energy connection, HBM, transformers, power electronics, cooling, and financing keep pace. The future of the AI economy depends as much on physical delivery discipline as on software intelligence.
The demand-side revolution

In classic software, a user opens an interface and issues a few commands. In the agent economy, the system plans dozens of steps in the background, calls different APIs, fails, retries, and verifies the result. Even if the unit token price falls, if the number of tasks and per-task consumption rise faster, the total bill grows — this is AI's own Jevons paradox.

The critical efficiency metric of the future won't be "price per million tokens," but total cost per successfully completed task.

The supply-side balance-sheet test

Data centers grow on multi-year contracts and large orders, while hardware's economic life is relatively short. If the revenue ramp is delayed, interest and depreciation keep accruing. Of two operators with the same capacity, the one with lower debt, secured energy, and a diversified customer base is far more resilient.

The more important question for 2026-2029 isn't the growth rate, but: how many months does it take for a dollar of CapEx to convert into billed revenue?

"Revolution" and "bubble" are not opposites. AI can be transformative in the long run while still producing mispriced companies and idle capacity in the short run.

💰 04 · Value Chain — Where Does the Profit Pool Flow?

▾

Demand growth alone doesn't enrich every layer equally. Scarcity, pricing power, capital needs, and switching costs determine how profit is distributed.

📶 Profit-Capture Power Index by Layer (0-100)
Energy/grid/cooling and HBM/packaging/optical networking are the layers with the highest scarcity and entry barriers; model providers and the data-center/neocloud layer are most exposed to price competition and debt risk.
The fastest growth isn't always the best investment economics. If an operator triples revenue while using even more CapEx and debt, it may not create shareholder value. Conversely, a slower-growing business with secure energy, contracts, and a sound capital structure can produce higher free cash flow.

▣ GPU utilization rate
The occupancy and billing rate of installed capacity shows real demand.
↻ Tokens per task

↗ CapEx / active revenue

🎯 06 · Decision Lens — Same Future, Different Actors

▾

The investor, the infrastructure operator, and the policymaker look at the same picture, but prioritize different risks.

Three filters for the investor
Balance sheet

Examine net debt, interest coverage, refinancing schedule, and depreciation burden before the growth rate.

Contract quality

Measure the backlog's cancellation terms, customer concentration, and when capacity will actually be billed.

Valuation

Don't treat a low-rate scenario as an automatic rally; if profit forecasts fall, multiple expansion alone may not be enough.

Three priorities for the operator
Task economics

Track total cost per completed task, error rate, and human-approval time — not the token price.

Capacity architecture

Make small-model routing, caching, queuing, and dynamic GPU allocation the default design.

Energy security

Manage grid connection, backup generation, cooling, and equipment supply as one system, not separate from software.

Three trade-offs for the policymaker
Grid planning

Design data centers as an energy actor capable of flexible load and on-site generation, not just a consumer.

Sovereign capacity

Tie public support not just to GPU purchases, but to domestic data, security, energy, and a skilled workforce.

Competition and society

Make transparent the impact of large infrastructure concentration on energy prices, water use, and small businesses.

🏁 08 · Conclusion — Neither Blind Optimism, Nor an Easy Bubble Narrative

▾

The AI cycle begins with a stronger profit base and higher barriers to entry than the dot-com era. But it also requires far more energy, debt, and physical infrastructure than earlier software cycles.

Where the bull case is right

If agents embed into real workflows, per-task cost falls, and energy capacity comes online on time, AI can create a long-lasting productivity and investment cycle. In that case, the strongest companies are the ones with control over distribution, contracts, energy, and their balance sheet — not just technology.

Where the bear case is right

If CapEx grows without converting into revenue, agent costs go uncontrolled, and low-rate expectations over-stretch valuations, the sector could see sharp corrections. That correction wouldn't mean AI is unnecessary — it would mean capital flowed to the wrong layer at the wrong price.

Final verdict: AI is probably not "a repeat of 2000"; but the most important lesson investors learned from 2000 still holds: technology changing the world doesn't mean every company and every price is right.
